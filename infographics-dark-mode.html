At a Glance

History of the M4 Sherman

Designed 1941 • First production Feb 1942 • Mass-production focus

Designed: 1941 — replacement for the M3 Lee/Grant.

Production: First rolled out Feb 1942 — welded or cast hulls, 75 mm main gun, reliable mechanics.

Built for scale: Mass production — >49,000 by war’s end (one of history’s most-produced tanks).

First Combat Use

British & US combat introduction — late 1942

El Alamein — October 1942 was the Sherman’s combat debut with British forces. Shortly after, U.S. units used it in Operation Torch (Nov 1942).

Key Variants for Modelers

Popular 1/35 kits & signature features

M4 / M4A1 (75 mm)

Early production; ideal for North Africa & early Normandy scenes — classic angled hull.

M4A3(76)W

Upgraded 76 mm gun — improved anti-tank performance for mid/late war builds.

M4A3E8 "Easy Eight"

HVSS suspension + 76 mm gun — smooth look, late-war Europe & Korea usage.

M4A3E2 "Jumbo"

Heavy-armour assault tank — great for assault or urban ruin dioramas.

Sherman Firefly

British conversion with 17-pounder — long barrel, turret mods, and unique markings.

(Modeling tip)

Mix parts from kits to create theatre-specific configurations (guns, stowage, turret mods).

Camouflage & Painting Schemes

Common finishes & weathering ideas for accuracy and drama

Desert / Dust

British Firefly
  • US Olive Drab: Most common; early vs late shades vary — crews often smudged/obscured white stars.
  • British Firefly Markings: Tactical numbers, divisional flashes, and distinct turret mods for the 17-pounder.
  • Winter & Desert: Whitewash for Ardennes; faded, sand-covered schemes in North Africa — excellent for weathering practice.
